Most videos are not lost at the two-minute mark. They are lost in the first three seconds. Whatever the platform, the viewer makes a fast decision — keep watching, or keep scrolling.

This post covers the editing choices that make people stay.

## Start at the most interesting moment

The single most common mistake is a slow build. Logo animation, a greeting, an explanation of what is coming. By the time anything happens, the viewer is gone.

Cut all of it. Open on the most interesting frame you have — the result, the reaction, the moment of tension — and let context arrive afterwards.

If your video is about a transformation, show the finished transformation first. If it is a story, open in the middle of the action.

## Give the eye motion immediately

A static opening frame reads as "nothing is happening here". Motion signals that something is in progress.

That can be a push-in on the subject, a whip transition into the scene, or simply starting on a moving shot rather than a locked-off one. It does not need to be dramatic — it needs to not be still.

## Put text on screen in the first second

Many people watch without sound, especially on Instagram and in public. If your hook only exists in the audio, a large share of viewers never receive it.

A few rules that work:

- Keep it under eight words
- Use large, high-contrast type
- Place it away from the platform's UI — the bottom of a Reel is covered by captions and buttons
- Make it a promise or a question the video will actually answer

## Cut faster than feels comfortable

In the opening seconds, trim every pause. Remove breaths, hesitations and the silence before someone starts speaking. What feels aggressively fast on the timeline usually feels normal to a viewer seeing it for the first time.

Once you are past the hook you can relax the pacing. The opening earns the attention that lets you slow down later.

## Use sound as a signal

A short riser, a soft impact on the first cut, or a clean whoosh into the opening shot gives the brain a reason to pay attention. Audio cues do a lot of the work that visuals get credit for.

Keep it subtle. An overloaded opening is as bad as an empty one.

## Deliver what the hook promised

This one matters more than any technique above. If the opening sets up a question, answer it. If it teases a result, show the result.

Clickbait that does not pay off produces a bounce, and platforms notice bounces. A strong hook with no payoff hurts more than a weak hook with a good video behind it.

## A quick checklist

Before exporting, check the first three seconds against these:

- Does something visually interesting happen immediately?
- Is there motion in the frame?
- Is there readable text for sound-off viewers?
- Is there a reason to keep watching?
- Does the video deliver on that reason?

If any answer is no, re-cut the opening. It is the highest-leverage part of the entire edit.
